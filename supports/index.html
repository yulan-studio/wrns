Canada Disability and Special Needs Supports and Funding Guide

A practical reference for families and caregivers: federal, provincial, and territorial income supports, child special needs programs, developmental disability services, assistive devices, respite, tax measures, and long-term savings supports.

Prepared: June 14, 2026 Language: English Sources: government and official program pages
Important note: eligibility rules, benefit amounts, waitlists, and application forms can change. Use this page as a starting point for finding the right program and building an application checklist. Before applying, confirm the latest amounts, residency rules, income and asset limits, medical documentation requirements, and application steps on the official program page.

Quick Start

Adults who cannot work or have low income because of a disability

  • Start with the disability income support program in your province or territory, such as ODSP, AISH, SAID, PWD, EIA, or Social Solidarity.
  • Also check federal programs: CPP Disability Benefit, Canada Disability Benefit, Disability Tax Credit, and RDSP.
  • If the person has a developmental disability, autism, or FASD, also look for adult developmental services such as CLBC, PDD, DSO/Passport, or DSP.

Children or youth with special needs

  • First confirm whether the child may qualify for the Disability Tax Credit, because it can unlock related federal supports.
  • Then check your province’s child special needs programs, such as Ontario ACSD/SSAH/OAP, Alberta FSCD, BC CYSN, or Manitoba Children’s disABILITY Services.
  • Ask the school about an IEP, special education assessments, therapy referrals, behaviour supports, and respite resources.

Equipment, modifications, or caregiving support

  • Look for assistive device and medical equipment programs, such as Ontario ADP, Alberta AADL, BC medical equipment programs, or your provincial health authority.
  • Search for home modification, vehicle modification, respite, community participation, and personal support funding.
  • Contact 211, a family doctor, school staff, a social worker, or a local disability service office for referrals.

Federal Government Programs

Program Who It May Help Support Provided Application / Entry Point
Canada Disability Benefit Generally intended for low-income persons with disabilities aged 18 to 64. Eligibility is closely connected to tax filing and, in many cases, the Disability Tax Credit. A federal direct income benefit intended to reduce poverty among working-age persons with disabilities. Amounts and eligibility are determined under current program rules. Canada Disability Benefit
Disability Tax Credit (DTC) People with severe and prolonged impairments in physical or mental functions. A medical practitioner completes the form and CRA reviews it. A non-refundable tax credit. It is also a key gateway to RDSP, the Child Disability Benefit, and other disability-related supports. DTC official page
Child Disability Benefit (CDB) Children under 18 who qualify for the DTC and whose families receive the Canada Child Benefit. A tax-free monthly benefit based on family net income. It is usually assessed automatically through the Canada Child Benefit system. Child Disability Benefit
CPP Disability Benefit People under 65 who have made enough CPP contributions and have a severe and prolonged disability that prevents regular work. A monthly taxable benefit. A related children’s benefit may be available for dependent children. CPP Disability Benefit
Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP) Canadian residents with a SIN who qualify for the DTC and meet age rules for opening or contributing to an RDSP. A long-term savings plan. Eligible plans may receive the Canada Disability Savings Grant and Bond. RDSP / Grant / Bond
Medical Expense Tax Credit and Caregiver-Related Tax Measures Persons with disabilities, caregivers, and families paying eligible medical, attendant care, accessibility, or disability-related expenses. May include medical expenses, attendant care, accessibility renovations, caregiver credits, and transferred disability amounts. Receipts and documentation are important. CRA disability tax overview
Opportunities Fund for Persons with Disabilities Employment service organizations, employers, and participating persons with disabilities. Employment preparation, skills training, job placement, and employer supports. Individuals usually access it through service providers. Opportunities Fund
Accessible Canada / Accessibility Programs Public, nonprofit, community, and employer projects. Individuals may benefit from accessibility improvements. Accessibility standards, project funding, community accessibility improvements, accessible information, and employment accessibility. Accessible Canada
Benefits Finder Anyone trying to screen for federal, provincial, or territorial benefits. A guided tool that filters benefits by location, age, family situation, income, disability, and employment status. Benefits Finder

Detailed Province and Territory Listings

Ontario Key programs: ODSP, ADP, ACSD, SSAH, OAP, Passport

Income SupportChildren’s Special NeedsAssistive DevicesDevelopmental Services

Main Programs

  • Ontario Disability Support Program (ODSP): income, employment, and health benefits for eligible adults with disabilities.
  • Assistive Devices Program (ADP): wheelchairs, mobility aids, hearing, respiratory, vision, and communication devices.
  • Assistance for Children with Severe Disabilities (ACSD): support for extra costs faced by families caring for a child with a severe disability.
  • Special Services at Home (SSAH): personal development and family respite supports for children and youth.
  • Ontario Autism Program (OAP): autism services for children and youth.
  • Passport Program and Developmental Services Ontario: community participation, respite, and personal supports for adults with developmental disabilities.

Application Tips

  • ODSP usually requires medical information, income and asset details, housing costs, and household information.
  • Start transition planning before age 18. Child programs, school supports, adult DSO/Passport, and ODSP may have separate waitlists and assessments.
  • For assistive devices, an authorized assessor or health professional often needs to complete an assessment before ADP funding can be requested.

British Columbia Key programs: PWD, CYSN, CLBC

Income SupportChildren and YouthCommunity Living
  • Disability Assistance / Persons with Disabilities (PWD): income and health-related supports for eligible people with PWD designation.
  • Children and Youth with Support Needs: entry point for child and youth special needs, family support, behaviour, and developmental services.
  • Autism Information and Services: autism diagnosis, funding, and service information.
  • Community Living BC (CLBC): community living, housing, employment, and life-skills services for eligible adults with developmental disabilities and some adults with FASD or ASD.
  • Travel Assistance Program: medical travel discounts or supports for eligible travel; also check local health authorities for equipment and home care services.

Alberta Key programs: AISH, FSCD, PDD, AADL

  • Assured Income for the Severely Handicapped (AISH): income and health benefits for adults with severe and permanent disabilities.
  • AISH benefits: living allowance, child benefit, health benefits, and personal benefits.
  • Family Support for Children with Disabilities (FSCD): family support, respite, equipment, and therapy-related services for children with disabilities.
  • Persons with Developmental Disabilities (PDD): home living, community access, employment, and personal supports for adults with developmental disabilities.
  • Alberta Aids to Daily Living (AADL): long-term medical equipment and supplies, such as hearing, wheelchair, respiratory, prosthetic, and ostomy supports.

Saskatchewan Key programs: SAID and cognitive disability supports

  • Saskatchewan Assured Income for Disability (SAID): income support for people with significant and enduring disabilities.
  • People with Disabilities: general disability services entry point.
  • Cognitive Disability Strategy: individualized supports, community participation, and family supports for people with cognitive disabilities.
  • Health services for people with disabilities: health, assistive device, and related service entry points.

Manitoba Key programs: EIA, Children’s disABILITY Services, CLDS

  • Employment and Income Assistance for Persons with Disabilities: basic needs, housing, and health-related supports for eligible persons with disabilities.
  • Rent Assist: rent support for low-income households, available inside and outside EIA depending on eligibility.
  • Children’s disABILITY Services: child development, respite, nursing, equipment, and family support.
  • Community Living disABILITY Services: residential, day, employment, and personal supports for adults with intellectual disabilities.
  • Manitoba Health programs: health coverage entry point for equipment, medications, and home care supports.

Quebec Key programs: Social Solidarity, CLSC/CIUSSS, child disability supplement

  • Social Assistance and Social Solidarity: entry point for income assistance and social solidarity programs.
  • Social Solidarity Program: income support for people with severely limited capacity for employment.
  • Services for people with a physical or intellectual disability or autism: CLSC/CIUSSS assessment, family support, rehabilitation, and community services.
  • Supplement for Handicapped Children: child disability supplement.
  • RAMQ aid programs: assistive devices, prosthetics, visual and hearing-related programs, and related supports.

New Brunswick Key programs: Disability Support Program, Social Assistance

  • Disability Support Program: individualized supports for adults with disabilities, including personal support, respite, community participation, assistive technology, and home supports.
  • Social Assistance: basic income support for people with low income; ask about disability-related add-ons where applicable.
  • Persons with Disabilities: general disability services entry point.
  • Health programs: entry point for medication, equipment, long-term care, and home support information.

Nova Scotia Key programs: DSP, ESIA, Flex / Individualized Funding

  • Disability Support Program (DSP): residential, family support, community participation, direct funding, and individualized supports.
  • Employment Support and Income Assistance (ESIA): income and employment supports for eligible people with low income.
  • Flex Individualized Funding: funding to purchase supports connected to an approved individual plan.
  • Department of Health and Wellness: home care, long-term care, and health equipment entry point.

Prince Edward Island Key program: AccessAbility Supports

  • AccessAbility Supports: personal support, respite, community participation, life skills, home and vehicle modifications, assistive devices, and assured income.
  • Social Assistance: basic support for people with low income.
  • Disability Supports: disability services and application entry point.
  • Home Care Program: home care, care assessments, and health supports.

Newfoundland and Labrador Key programs: Income Support, NL Disability Benefit, health-related services

  • Income Support: basic needs, housing, health-related services, child benefits, and support trusts.
  • Disability Policy Office: disability policy, accessibility, and related program information.
  • Newfoundland and Labrador Disability Benefit: provincial disability benefit entry point.
  • Health Related Services: medical, medication, and related cost supports for income support recipients.
  • Support Trusts: rules for disability-related assets and trust arrangements.

Yukon Key programs: Social Assistance, Supplementary Allowance

  • Social Assistance: basic support for people with low income.
  • Yukon Supplementary Allowance: support for low-income people who cannot work because of age, disability, or related reasons.
  • Social supports: entry point for family, child, income, and disability-related supports.
  • Care services: home care, long-term care, and community health services.

Northwest Territories Key programs: Income Assistance, Income Security, disability services

  • Income Assistance: basic needs, housing, utilities, child, and special needs supports.
  • Income Security Programs: general income security entry point.
  • Services for Persons with Disabilities: health and social services entry point for persons with disabilities.
  • Home and Community Care: home and community care services.

Nunavut Key programs: Income Assistance, local community offices

  • Income Assistance: basic income support, usually accessed through the local community social services office.
  • Family Services: entry point for family, income, and social services.
  • Department of Health: health, travel, home, and community health service information.
  • Programs and Services: program lookup and local office information.

Documents to Prepare Before Applying

  • Identification: SIN, birth certificate, passport, PR card or citizenship document, health card, and proof of provincial or territorial residence.
  • Medical documentation: diagnosis reports, functional limitation descriptions, psychological or educational assessments, and forms completed by doctors, therapists, nurse practitioners, or other authorized professionals.
  • Income and assets: Notice of Assessment, bank statements, wages, insurance, CPP-D, EI, spouse or partner income, rent or mortgage costs, and utility bills.
  • Child documents: school IEP, psychoeducational assessment, speech/occupational/physical therapy reports, behaviour plans, caregiving time, and records of extra costs.
  • Equipment or modifications: professional assessments, quotes, prescriptions, supplier information, photos, and repair or replacement records.
  • Caregiving records: respite needs, family caregiving hours, overnight care, crisis incidents, barriers to community participation, and transportation needs.

Application Strategy

  1. Start with the federal DTC where appropriate: if approved, it may make CDB, RDSP, Canada Disability Benefit, and some other disability-related funding easier to access.
  2. Apply for income supports and service supports separately: many provinces run income assistance, developmental services, child special needs, and assistive device programs through different departments.
  3. Plan the transition to adulthood 12 to 24 months early: child benefits, school supports, adult developmental services, substitute decision-making or authorization, and income supports may each have their own assessment process.
  4. Keep receipts and documentation: tax credits, medical expenses, respite, transportation, equipment, and accessibility renovations often require proof.
  5. Use 211 and local service navigation to fill gaps: beyond government programs, many communities have food, transportation, day programs, legal aid, mental health, and caregiver support groups.
